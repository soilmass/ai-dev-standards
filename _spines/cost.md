# Cost & FinOps Spine

Cost is not a layer — it's a non-functional requirement every layer can quietly blow, and for a solo operator an unbounded bill or a denial-of-wallet attack is an availability incident. This spine doesn't duplicate the layer rules; it shows **where cost bites in each layer**, owns the cross-cutting framing (cost as a budgeted, observed metric), and points at the limits and signals that keep spend bounded.

## Where it bites, layer by layer

| Layer | Cost obligation | Lives in |
|---|---|---|
| 02 Product | A feature that costs per-use (paid API, LLM, large compute) states its expected unit economics in the spec — "what does one use cost us?" | `02-product/spec.template.md`, `02-product/acceptance-criteria.md` |
| 03 Design | Expensive/metered endpoints carry a **cost-based limit** (not just a request count); architecture avoids per-request fan-out to paid services | `03-design/rate-limiting-abuse.md` |
| 04 Build | Dependencies are justified against their weight (install/transitive/runtime cost); no needless paid-service calls | `04-build/dependency-policy.md` |
| 06 Delivery | Hosting model is chosen with its cost shape in mind (serverless per-invocation vs. warm container) | `00-governance/pinned-decisions.md`, `06-delivery/deployment-strategy.md` |
| 07 Operations | Cost is **observed**: metered third-party call volume and spend are emitted and alerted, like any other golden signal | `07-operations/observability.md` |
| 08 Maintenance | Unused paid deps/services are pruned; accepted-cost trade-offs are re-confirmed on the quarterly sweep | `04-build/dependency-policy.md` (quarterly prune), `08-maintenance/tech-debt-policy.md` |

## Cross-cutting rules owned here

1. **An uncapped expensive endpoint is a denial-of-wallet vulnerability.** Any path that triggers a paid third-party/LLM call, a large export, an email/SMS send, or heavy compute must be **cost-budgeted at the boundary** — by a per-user/per-key cost or token bucket, a concurrency cap, or a hard quota (`03-design/rate-limiting-abuse.md` rule 3). This is the same OWASP "unrestricted resource consumption" risk as a DoS, pointed at the bill instead of the CPU.
2. **Cost is a metric you emit from day one, not a surprise on the invoice.** Metered third-party/LLM call volume (and, where the provider exposes it, spend) is an observable signal with a threshold alert (`07-operations/observability.md` rule 11) — a vendor silently 10×-ing your usage or an abuse spike should page on *cost burn*, not be discovered at month-end. You can't bound what you don't measure.
3. **Every dependency and integration has a cost, and it's part of the decision.** Adding a library carries install/transitive/runtime weight (`04-build/dependency-policy.md`); adopting a paid SaaS carries a recurring bill and a per-call rate. The ADR that adopts a runtime integration (`04-build/third-party-integrations.md`) names its cost model and a kill-switch, so a cost spike has an off-ramp.
4. **Pick the cost shape deliberately, then right-size.** The hosting decision (`00-governance/pinned-decisions.md`'s three-box model) is partly a cost-shape decision — serverless trades a low floor for per-invocation cost that a hot path can blow, while a warm container trades a fixed floor for cheap throughput. Choose for the workload, and don't over-provision "just in case."
5. **Prune what you don't use.** Unimported dependencies, idle paid services, and over-broad provisioned capacity are recurring cost with no value; the quarterly dependency prune and debt sweep (`08-maintenance/*`) re-confirm that every recurring cost still earns its place.

## The three phases, mapped to the rules

The FinOps operating model runs cost through a continuous loop — **Inform → Optimize → Operate** — and a solo operator runs the same loop in miniature. Rules 1–5 already live in those phases; naming the loop makes the gaps visible.

6. **Inform: make spend legible before you act on it.** You cannot optimize what you cannot attribute. Tag/label every metered call, deployed resource, and paid integration with the feature or surface that owns it, so spend rolls up to a thing you can decide about (kill it, cap it, accept it) rather than an undifferentiated invoice line. Rule 2 (emit cost as a metric) and the per-call attribution in rule 7 are the Inform phase; rule 1's spec-stated unit economics is Inform done at design time.
7. **Optimize: right-size and cap against the attributed picture.** The cost-shape choice (rule 4), the boundary cost budget (rule 1), and the prune (rule 5) are the Optimize phase — they act on what Inform exposed. Optimize is recurring, not one-shot: a workload that was cheap serverless at low volume can cross the break-even into wanting a warm box, and the quarterly sweep is where that re-decision happens.
8. **Operate: hold the loop on a cadence, with an owner.** For a solo operator the owner is you, and the cadence is the existing review rhythm — cost burn is glanced at on release alongside the error budget (`07-operations/slo-error-budgets.md` rule 9), and recurring cost is re-confirmed on the quarterly debt sweep (`08-maintenance/tech-debt-policy.md`). Operate is what keeps Inform and Optimize from being a one-time launch exercise that rots.

## Emitting a cost signal & alerting on it

9. **A cost signal is emitted the same way any golden signal is — per unit of work, with attribution.** For a metered or paid call (notably an LLM call, which is billed per token in *and* out), record the cost dimension at the call site: token counts and the model/endpoint used, tagged with the owning feature and, where it matters, the user or key (an opaque ID, never PII — `_spines/security-privacy.md`). Where the provider exposes a cost/usage API, reconcile the emitted estimate against the billed actual on a cadence so drift between "what we think we spend" and "what we're billed" surfaces early rather than at month-end. The estimate-at-call-site is the fast signal; the provider API is the source of truth.
10. **Cost-anomaly alerting is a signal peer to SLO error-budget burn, and is shaped like it.** A sudden multiple of baseline spend — an abuse spike, a runaway loop, a vendor silently re-pricing or 10×-ing usage — should *page on cost burn*, not be discovered on the invoice (`07-operations/observability.md` rule 11). Borrow the burn-rate shape from reliability (`07-operations/slo-error-budgets.md` rule 5): alert on the *rate* of spend relative to budget, not a raw monthly total, so both a hard spike and a slow bleed trip it. The fast signal pages; the reconciled provider total backstops it.
11. **An optional cost SLO turns "is the bill OK?" from a feeling into a target.** Where unit economics matter, set a cost objective the same way you set a reliability SLO — a ceiling on a normalized ratio (cost per active user, cost per thousand requests, cost per successful job), not an absolute dollar figure that scales with growth and tells you nothing. Set it from measured reality (run a month, read the data) and treat a breach the way you treat a burnt error budget: the next unit of work is cost work — find the regression, cap the path, re-decide the cost shape — before more features. A normalized cost SLO is what lets healthy growth (more users, more spend, flat per-user cost) read as fine while a leak (flat users, rising per-user cost) reads as the incident it is.

## Idle-resource audit

12. **Idle and orphaned resources are the silent half of the bill — audit for them on the prune cadence.** Right-sizing what you use (rule 4) does nothing about what you've stopped using but never tore down. The quarterly sweep (rule 5) walks a concrete list:
    - **Orphaned storage** — detached/unattached volumes, old disk snapshots and machine images, buckets full of stale exports/build artifacts with no lifecycle expiry, abandoned backup tiers past their retention ladder (`07-operations/backup-dr.md`).
    - **Idle databases & caches** — non-production or experiment instances left running, an over-provisioned managed tier serving a fraction of its capacity, read replicas nothing reads.
    - **Unused provisioned capacity** — reserved/committed capacity bought for a load that never materialized, idle compute, generously-sized functions, paid plan tiers above actual usage.
    - **Dangling credentials & endpoints** — API keys and tokens for integrations you removed (a cost *and* a security surface — they still bill and still authenticate), unused static IPs/load balancers, leftover preview/branch environments, zombie scheduled jobs that run and bill but feed nothing.

## Standards basis

- **FinOps Foundation — FinOps Framework** (finops.org/framework) — the operating model whose three iterative phases — **Inform** (visibility & allocation: see and attribute spend), **Optimize** (rates & usage: right-size, eliminate waste, re-decide), **Operate** (continuous improvement & cloud governance: run the loop on a cadence with an owner) — are the basis for rules 6–8 mapping the existing rules onto that loop. The framework's principle that **engineers take ownership of the cost of what they build**, its **resource-tagging/allocation** discipline (Inform — basis for rules 6 and 9's attribution), and its **anomaly-detection** practice (basis for rule 10) ground rules 2–5 and 9–12 as cost-as-an-owned-observed-continuously-reviewed metric scaled to a solo operator.
- **OWASP API Security Top 10 — API4:2023 Unrestricted Resource Consumption** (owasp.org/API-Security) — explicitly names the financial dimension ("can lead to … increased operational costs"), i.e. **denial-of-wallet**; the basis for rule 1. Shares the limit mechanics with `03-design/rate-limiting-abuse.md`.
- **AWS Well-Architected Framework — Cost Optimization pillar** (aws.amazon.com/architecture/well-architected) — its design principles are the cloud-vendor form of this spine: **implement cloud financial management** (the Operate cadence, rule 8), **adopt a consumption model** / **measure overall efficiency** (pay for what you use and track a normalized cost-per-outcome — the basis for the cost-shape choice in rule 4 and the cost SLO in rule 11), **attribute expenditure** (cost allocation via tags — Inform, rules 6 and 9), and **stop spending on undifferentiated heavy lifting**. Its right-sizing and elimination-of-waste guidance grounds rule 4 (match capacity to demand, per-invocation vs. fixed-floor trade-offs) and the idle-resource audit in rule 12. (The GCP/Azure Well-Architected cost pillars carry the same right-sizing and waste-elimination guidance as a cross-vendor corroboration.)
- **Google SRE — error-budget burn-rate alerting** (*SRE Workbook*, "Alerting on SLOs", https://sre.google/workbook/alerting-on-slos/) — rules 10–11 deliberately borrow reliability's shape: cost-anomaly alerting is burn-rate alerting pointed at the bill (alert on *rate* relative to budget, not an absolute total, so a spike and a slow bleed both trip), and the optional cost SLO is the SLI/SLO/budget model applied to a normalized cost ratio. The shape is owned and cited in full at `07-operations/slo-error-budgets.md`; this spine reuses it for cost.
- Builds on `03-design/rate-limiting-abuse.md` (cost limits — owned there), `04-build/dependency-policy.md` (dependency weight), `04-build/third-party-integrations.md` (integration cost + kill-switch), `07-operations/observability.md` (cost as a signal, alerting), and `07-operations/slo-error-budgets.md` (the burn-rate/SLO shape rules 10–11 reuse). This spine names cost as a first-class, budgeted, observed concern across them rather than an afterthought.

## Enforcement
- Mechanism: none-possible
- Config: n/a
- Fallback if unenforceable: Expensive or metered operations (paid third-party/LLM calls, large exports, fan-out, email/SMS) carry a cost budget or limit at the boundary and emit an observable cost signal; new dependencies and paid integrations are justified against their cost; no endpoint is an uncapped denial-of-wallet surface.

## Bootstrap
- What new-project.sh injects for this standard: nothing — reference only (cost budgets, the chosen hosting cost-shape, and the cost-metric vendor are per-project choices; the rate-limit, dependency-policy, and observability surfaces this spine points at are injected by their own standards).
